Bait and Switch

The rod weighs lightly in your hand as you pick it up. Reel is attached, string taut.

At picking up the rod, the door whips open. It is not as hardy a door as you'd thought, appearing to be made out of canvas. It flaps closed, is plucked by the wind, opens again, showing bright sun and the unmarred surface of a lake.

The tin, upon opening, is full of teeth. Not newly pulled, aged yellow. You cannot distinguish from what creature they've come.

Out the window is blackness.

The Fisher

That looks to be my fishing rod.  Thank you very much for the food and water.  

You look to the fisherman.

What else can you tell me?

The fisherman seems strangled in his words; he holds the rod from the center and it bows on either side.

He looks back at the bearded man, doubtfully, and departs; the other man closes the door behind him.

He rubs at his wrist and mutters a few unhearable syllables. A stool disassembled levitates through the window, reassembles itself before him and he sits, gliding towards you.

Fisher, he says, your precision astounds me. Of all. He pauses and spits in his hand, rubs it against his other, and then through his hair.

It wasn't I who summoned you. Aloste is responsible for that. And she who suggested that we needed you. I simply scry and report.

And I test, he says, looking wistfully out the window. Though some mornings it seems fruitless, inane even, he coughs into a palm, there is a greater risk though of sending someone incapable into The Mog'r. Guilt.

Time for your test, it seems, Fisher. Speed of Rule to you. Catch many.

His eyes burn down into yours, a darkness spreading from the pupils outward across the white and with a slight chant, he tosses a small powder across your face and with an unexpected lurch, the world blackens, flashes, and you are alone in the room.

The fishing rod and a small tin are at your knees.

Starving

Yes, I'm starving, thank you.

The fisherman nods and the man at the door knocks on it three times.

You gulp from the canteen, surprised at how thirsty you actually are.

Please tell me, where am I?  What happened? I don't remember much.

As best as they've told me, boyo, we're a ways up in the trees. Someplace, I can't pronounce it so well. The old man picks at his teeth. But I came with you, he says, shrugging.

You've been out for a few days, he says, laughing a little as you sputter up more water. Thats why you're so thirsty.

The door opens and a plate of pig, wild-rice and beans is handed in. The young man hands it to the fisherman who in turn places it in front of you.

They'll treat you alright, he says quietly. Seems, anyway.

But they want to know what this is, he holds it out in front of you– the fishing rod which at first you presumed was his but on closer inspection you recognize as your own.

Hello, Leon

An old fisherman, rod in hand, scratches at the underside of his stomach as he strolls in.

He asks you how you're doing, not seeming to particularly run it as a question, more a greeting.

Imagine you're thirsty. You've been under a few days now, he says, setting the rod against the wall and walking a canteen over to you.

A young man with wispy beard and crooked eyes steps in the door and stands at its entrance. He holds a slim stick, is acknowledged by the fisherman.

Care for something to eat? the fisherman asks.
